# Composite Construction & Weight Optimization: Otto & Dark Aero Lessons

**Research Date:** 2026-04-03  
**Researcher:** CHAIRMAN  
**Purpose:** Document proven composite construction techniques and weight optimization strategies from Otto Aerospace and Dark Aero for application to MAOS aircraft structures.

---

## Executive Summary

**Otto Aerospace** achieves weight optimization indirectly through drag reduction enabled by precision composite manufacturing (resin transfer molding, ultra-smooth surfaces). Their laminar flow airframe requires tighter-than-standard tolerances and advanced surface finishing.

**Dark Aero** achieves direct weight savings through structural innovation—specifically their "Hollow Grid" wing design, which eliminates traditional spar/rib construction in favor of a distributed cellular structure, saving ~10 lbs per wing (~10% reduction) while adding fuel capacity.

Both builders prioritize carbon fiber composites, tight manufacturing control, and structural efficiency over brute strength.

---

## Otto Aerospace: Precision Manufacturing for Laminar Flow

### Construction Techniques

**Resin Transfer Molding (RTM):**
- Primary method for wings, fuselage, and major structural components
- Resin injected into molds containing carbon fiber preforms
- Same process used by Airbus for A220 production
- Enables high-precision, repeatable production at scale
- Minimizes surface distortions that disrupt laminar flow

**Precision Tooling:**
- Partner Re:Build Manufacturing produces composite control surfaces with tightly toleranced assemblies
- Thermoset composites ensure structural integrity while maintaining ultra-smooth external surfaces
- Tolerances tighter than conventional aviation standards to prevent micro-disturbances in airflow
- Specific numerical values (Ra surface roughness) not publicly disclosed, but described as "next-generation precision"

**Surface Engineering:**
- Ultra-smooth finishes comparable to stealth aircraft manufacturing
- Prevents micro-imperfections that trigger turbulence and transition to turbulent flow
- Windowless fuselage design eliminates surface discontinuities
- All-composite airframe supports larger cabin while reducing structural weight

### Weight Optimization Strategy

Otto's approach to weight reduction is **indirect but powerful:**

1. **Drag reduction = lighter propulsion requirements**
   - 35% lower drag than comparable jets
   - 61% less fuel burn
   - Enables smaller engines, lighter fuel tanks, reduced MTOW

2. **Laminar flow across entire airframe**
   - 90%+ laminar flow on wings even at Mach 0.80 with 23° sweep
   - Slotted airfoils maintain laminar attachment
   - Windowless fuselage prevents flow disruption

3. **Structural efficiency through manufacturing precision**
   - RTM produces consistent, high-strength parts with minimal material waste
   - Tight tolerances reduce need for post-production rework or added structural reinforcement

**Key Insight:** Otto doesn't chase weight savings through exotic materials or aggressive design—they achieve lightness by making drag so low that the entire propulsion system shrinks.

### Manufacturing Scalability

- Pre-production airframes built via Re:Build Manufacturing
- Full-scale production planned for Florida facility
- RTM process supports volume production (Airbus-validated method)
- Composite layups and resin types not publicly detailed beyond "thermosets and carbon fiber"

## Dark Aero: Structural Innovation for Direct Weight Savings

### Hollow Grid Wing Structure

**What It Replaces:**
- Traditional I-beam spars (or C-shaped composite spars)
- Foam-core ribs (multiple molded components)

**What It Uses:**
- **Spar caps integrated directly into wing skins** (upper and lower surfaces)
- **Airfoil-shaped ribs** cut from honeycomb panels via CNC router
- **Shear webs** added after bonding ribs to skins
- Creates a hollow, distributed grid structure resembling a cellular skeleton

**Weight Savings:**
- **~10 lbs per wing** (20 lbs total for both wings)
- ~10% reduction on a ~100 lb wing
- Eliminates labor-intensive rib molding (saves at least a dozen molds per wing)

**Structural Benefits:**
- Spar caps in skins provide buckling resistance without separate spar structure
- Distributed load paths reduce stress concentrations
- Validated for +3.8/-1.5G loads (high-speed, long-range flight envelope)
- Hollow interior adds ~5 gallons fuel capacity per wing (range extension)

**Manufacturing Advantages:**
- CNC-routed honeycomb ribs: repeatable, fast, low labor
- Fewer molds required (major cost/time savings)
- Automation-friendly for mass production
- Reduces manual layup work compared to traditional composite rib construction

### Composite Manufacturing Processes

Dark Aero teaches and uses multiple methods depending on application:

**Wet Layup:**
- Dry carbon fiber cloth saturated with resin via brush/roller, then cured
- **Pros:** Simple, low-cost, minimal tooling
- **Cons:** Resin-rich (heavier), variable quality, requires higher safety factors
- **Best for:** Non-critical structures, prototyping

**Prepreg:**
- Pre-impregnated fibers with precise resin ratio
- **Pros:** Consistent quality, reduced errors, preferred for aviation certification
- **Cons:** Shelf life limits, minimum order quantities, higher cost
- **Best for:** Primary structures (wings, fuselage, spars)

**Assembly:**
- Components bonded with epoxy adhesive (ribs to skins, shear webs, etc.)
- Mold-based production for wings (dedicated tooling, stored in factory)
- Foam-core alternatives explored but not primary method

### Design Philosophy

**Composite-Specific Thinking:**
- Fiber orientation optimization for bending, shear, fatigue (anisotropic properties vs. isotropic metals)
- High strength-to-weight ratio enables aggressive weight reduction
- Validated via simulation + physical testing (no guess-and-check)

**Integration Over Addition:**
- Spar caps in skins = dual-purpose structure (no separate spar + skin attachment)
- Hollow grid = structure + fuel tankage
- Every component serves multiple functions where possible

**Target Performance:**
- DarkAero 1 empty weight: ~750 lbs
- Wing weight: ~100 lbs each (200 lbs total)
- Hollow grid contributes to overall weight efficiency alongside fuselage/systems integration

## Key Lessons for MAOS Structures

### Manufacturing Approach

1. **Precision matters for laminar flow** (Otto lesson)
   - If MAOS pursues laminar flow wing or fuselage sections, tight manufacturing tolerances are non-negotiable
   - RTM or similar controlled processes preferred over hand layup for repeatable surface finish
   - Surface roughness must be minimized to prevent premature flow transition

2. **Structural innovation > material exotic-ness** (Dark Aero lesson)
   - Hollow grid wing structure saves 10% weight without advanced materials
   - Distributed load paths and integrated spar caps eliminate redundant structure
   - CNC-routed honeycomb ribs: fast, repeatable, automation-ready

3. **Process selection depends on criticality**
   - Prepreg for primary structures (wings, fuselage, load-bearing components)
   - Wet layup acceptable for secondary structures (fairings, non-critical panels)
   - Mold investment pays off for repeated components (wings, control surfaces)

### Weight Optimization Strategies

1. **Direct savings via structural design** (Dark Aero)
   - Hollow grid wing: 10 lbs per wing
   - Integrated spar caps: eliminate separate spar structure
   - Cellular rib design: lighter than foam-core, better load distribution

2. **Indirect savings via drag reduction** (Otto)
   - Laminar flow reduces drag → smaller engines, less fuel, lower MTOW
   - Precision manufacturing enables aerodynamic efficiency
   - System-level weight savings compound over entire airframe

3. **Manufacturing efficiency = weight efficiency**
   - Fewer molds = less tooling weight, faster iteration
   - CNC automation reduces human error, improves consistency
   - Scalable processes (RTM, CNC routing) support volume production without quality loss

### Composite Material Constraints

- **Temperature limits:** ~120°C max for composite structures (resin degradation above this)
- **Anisotropic properties:** Fiber orientation critical for load paths (not isotropic like metal)
- **Lightning strike protection:** May require conductive mesh in composite skins
- **Inspection/repair:** Damage detection harder than metal, repair methods differ

## Application to MAOS

**If MAOS pursues laminar flow (AERO decision):**
- Consider RTM or similar controlled composite process (not hand layup)
- Budget for precision tooling and tight tolerance control
- Surface finish requirements drive manufacturing method selection

**For wing structures:**
- Hollow grid design warrants serious consideration (10% weight savings, proven at +3.8G)
- CNC-routed honeycomb ribs faster and lighter than molded composite ribs
- Integrated spar caps in skins eliminate separate spar → fewer parts, lower weight

**For fuselage structures:**
- Composite monocoque construction typical for homebuilt/kit aircraft
- Consider moldless construction methods if production volume low (foam-core plug, layup, trim)
- If volume production planned, invest in fuselage molds (faster cycle time, repeatable quality)

**Manufacturing process hierarchy (by structural criticality):**
1. **Primary structures** (wing spar caps, fuselage shell): Prepreg or RTM
2. **Secondary structures** (ribs, bulkheads, fairings): Wet layup or CNC-cut honeycomb
3. **Non-structural** (interior panels, cowlings): Wet layup, foam-core, or fiberglass
