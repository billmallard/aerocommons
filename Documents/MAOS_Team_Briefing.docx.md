# **1\.  What We Are Building**

MAOS is a four-seat, IFR-capable, series-hybrid experimental airplane being designed and built under FAA Experimental Amateur-Built rules (the 51% rule). It is a clean-sheet design that draws on Burt Rutan’s philosophy of inherent aerodynamic safety and Jim Bede’s insistence that a determined amateur should be able to build it in a two-car garage.

The project is currently in preliminary design. No metal has been cut. This is the moment when additional perspective has the most leverage.

# **2\.  The Fuselage — Pod-and-Boom**

The fuselage is a pod-and-boom configuration. The ‘pod’ is a self-contained pressure vessel carrying the crew and all systems. The ‘boom’ is a structural tube connecting the pod to the tail. The two are different structures serving different jobs, and separating them simplifies both.

## **Why a Pod**

A conventional fuselage tapers from a wide mid-section to a narrow tail. That taper wastes cabin space, complicates frame construction, and forces the designer to run systems around structural compromises. A cylindrical pod avoids all of that:

* Constant cross-section — every frame is the same part. Fabrication is repetitive, not unique.

* Pressure vessel geometry — a cylinder is the optimal shape for pressurization loads. No corners, no stress risers.

* 50-inch interior width — comparable to a Cessna 210\. Two adults in the rear do not share a kidney.

* All systems (electrical, avionics, environmental) are accessible from inside the pod during build and maintenance.

* Fire separation built in — the generators are in the wing, not in the same compartment as passengers.

## **Why a Boom**

The tail boom is a simple structural tube — extruded aluminum or composite. It carries tail loads back to the pod and, in this design, also carries the propulsion system at its aft end. It is the ideal structure for that job because a tube in bending and torsion is weight-efficient, and a tube in tension (from propulsion thrust) is the most efficient use of a tube that exists.

The boom attaches to the pod at a single bolted flange (Station FS144). Remove eight bolts and the boom separates from the pod — relevant for shipping, storage, and trailer integration.

# **3\.  The Wing — Removable, With the Engines Inside It**

The wing is a one-piece structure that lifts off the fuselage as a single unit. The generators — two small ICE engines running in pure generator mode — are mounted inside the wing center section, flush with the upper skin. When the wing comes off the airplane for trailering, the generators come with it. They remain wired to their respective coolant loops. The wing is a self-contained energy module.

## **Generator Installation in the Wing**

Each generator engine sits in the wing center section between the spars, with its axis running roughly fore-to-aft. This is not a novel concept — it is how the Dornier Seastar and several experimental designs handle overwing powerplants. The advantages for MAOS are substantial:

* No external nacelle — zero nacelle drag. The upper wing surface has a flush NACA inlet and a lower-surface exhaust exit. That is the complete external signature.

* Fire separation — the generators are outboard of the pressure vessel and above the wing, not in the same bay as passengers.

* Structural efficiency — the engine mass hangs at the wing’s strongest point, the spar carry-through, where the structure already exists to handle large loads.

* Accessibility — removable upper wing skin panels allow full generator access for maintenance. No cowl to remove, no special tooling.

* Wing-off servicing — when the wing is on the trailer, both generators are fully accessible at table height. No ladders, no craning your neck into a hot engine bay.

## **The Crane and Trailer Integration**

The trailer is not a passive carrier. It is an active ground support system. The wing does not lift off by hand or by rented crane. It lifts off by a small davit crane that is part of the trailer structure.

The sequence for deployment from the trailer:

1\.  Trailer is positioned alongside the fuselage pod on the airstrip.

2\.  Crane arm deploys from trailer — a simple davit on a mast, no hydraulics.

3\.  Lifting bridle attaches to four hard points at the spar carry-through.

4\.  Wing lifts clear of the trailer deck, swings over the pod.

5\.  Wing saddle locates onto the carry-through fitting on the pod. Four bolts.

6\.  Electrical connectors and coolant quick-disconnects are plugged in.

7\.  Struts pin in. Total elapsed time with one person: approximately 45 minutes.

| THE KEY REQUIREMENT One pilot. One trailer. Any flat strip. No FBO, no hangar lease, no ground crew, no tow vehicle for the airplane. The wing crane is part of the design from the first sketched line — not a retrofit. |

# **4\.  Propulsion — Series Hybrid Architecture**

This is the element of the design that is most unconventional and most worth understanding. The propulsion system is a series hybrid — the same fundamental architecture that has powered diesel-electric locomotives for 80 years and nuclear submarines since the 1950s.

## **How It Works**

The ICE engines never touch the drivetrain. They run in pure generator mode at a single fixed RPM and power setting — maximum efficiency, always. All thrust comes from twin electric rim-drive motors on the tail boom. The generators charge a small battery buffer that covers peak demand and provides emergency reserve.

| **Generator output** | \~120 HP combined continuous electrical output |

| **Propulsion** | Dual contra-rotating rim-drive electric motors, tail boom |
| **Motor supplier** | Beyond Motors AXM3 (Slovenia) — 220 kW peak, 27 kg each |
| **Contra-rotation** | Zero net torque. No P-factor. No Vmc. Motors cancel each other. |
| **Folding props** | Prop blades fold aft when unpowered. Glide ratio: \~17:1 from 17,500 ft. |
| **Altitude** | Full electric motor power at any altitude. Generators de-rate gracefully. |
| **Fuel** | Pump gasoline — mogas, same as your car |

## **Why This Matters to a Pilot**

Three things about this architecture that every pilot in this neighborhood should find interesting:

* No Vmc. None. Both motors are on the centerline. Losing one generator reduces thrust symmetrically. There is no asymmetric thrust condition that exists in this airplane at any airspeed. The accident mode that kills people in conventional twins on departure is not available to this airplane.

* Engine failure on takeoff is not an emergency. Generator fails at Vr — battery sustains both motors, both props keep turning, thrust reduces gradually. Pilot has minutes, not seconds. The failure mode goes from violent and immediate to managed and gradual.

* Full power at any altitude. Electric motors are not affected by air density. The generator de-rates with altitude. The battery covers the difference during climb. At 17,500 feet you have the same thrust as at sea level.

# **5\.  Failure Analysis — Why the Redundancy Is Real**

The twin-generator, twin-motor, cross-tied bus architecture was chosen specifically for its failure mode behavior. Below is the complete single-failure matrix. ‘Manageable’ means reduced performance, no emergency handling required, proceed to nearest airport normally.

| FAILURE EVENT | SYSTEM RESPONSE | OUTCOME |
| ----- | ----- | :---: |
| **Generator A fails** | System B powers BOTH motors at reduced thrust | **MANAGEABLE** |
| **Generator B fails** | System A powers BOTH motors at reduced thrust | **MANAGEABLE** |
| **Motor 1 (forward) fails** | Gen A feeds Motor 2 via cross-tie bus | **MANAGEABLE** |
| **Motor 2 (aft) fails** | Gen B feeds Motor 1 via cross-tie bus | **MANAGEABLE** |
| **Generator \+ Motor, same side** | Surviving generator \+ motor via bus tie | **MANAGEABLE** |
| **BOTH generators fail** | Battery sustains both motors — \~10 min to land | **CONTROLLED** |
| **All propulsion fails** | Folding props retract, glide 50 mi from 17,500 ft | **SURVIVABLE** |

The ‘both generators fail simultaneously’ case requires two independent fuel systems, two independent engines, and two independent generator heads to all fail within the same flight — a scenario that is statistically improbable and would likely indicate a fuel contamination event affecting both tanks, at which point the aircraft has other problems.

The ‘all propulsion fails’ case is a full glide scenario. With folding props, the airplane has approximately a 17:1 glide ratio. At 17,500 feet service ceiling that is a glide radius of roughly 50 miles. This is the terrain clearance that makes dead-stick landings on prepared surfaces achievable from almost any point in the cruise flight envelope.
